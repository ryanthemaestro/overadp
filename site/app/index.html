OverADP — How to Use the War Room

OverADP is an AI-powered fantasy football draft assistant. It uses machine learning trained on 7 years of NFL data plus pre-season depth-chart snapshots, teammate target competition, and conformalized quantile regression for calibrated confidence intervals. Updated April 2026 with depth-chart awareness and CQR intervals.

Understanding the UI

Player Pool (Left Panel)

Each player row has two action buttons:

  • DRAFT — Click to add this player to YOUR team.
  • TAKEN — Click to mark this player as drafted by an OPPONENT (removes them from the board).
ColumnMeaning
VBDValue Based Drafting — projected points above the replacement level at each position. Higher = more valuable relative to a waiver-wire fill-in.
PROJProjected fantasy points for the full season (half-PPR by default; scoring format is configurable).
CIConformalized 80% Confidence Interval [low–high]. Validated on held-out seasons: when we say the 80% CI is 110–180 pts, the truth actually lands in that range ~80% of the time. Rookies have wider CIs (1.8× widening); second-year players 1.3×.
RISKlow / medium / high — computed as a position-relative quartile on relative CI width (width ÷ projection). Low = stable like Josh Allen / Chase / Gibbs. High = wide CI or near-replacement-level depth.
BYEBye week for this player's team. Watch for conflicts with your other starters.
ADPAverage Draft Position from Fantasy Football Calculator — where the market values this player. Sleepers = model rank better than ADP; busts = worse.

Draft Board Sort

Offensive players (QB/RB/WR/TE) are sorted by VBD (value over replacement) and interleaved by position within each tier, so you don't see 50 RBs in a row — the order mimics how a real draft flows. Kickers (K) and Defenses (DEF) are sorted by projected points since they have no meaningful VBD signal.

My Team (Center Panel)

Roster slots are driven by your league configuration (Settings). Players are auto-assigned in priority order:

  • QB → QB slot(s), then Superflex (if configured)
  • RB → RB1, RB2 (or however many RB slots), then Flex, then Bench
  • WR → WR slots, then Flex, then Bench
  • TE → TE slot(s), then Flex, then Bench
  • K / DEF → their dedicated slots (configurable count)

Bye weeks are shown next to each player. Red = bye conflict with another starter at the same position.

Key Concepts

VBD (Value Based Drafting)

VBD = Projected Points − Replacement Level Points. The replacement level is the projected points of the last player typically drafted at each position (including bench). This means even bench-worthy players get positive VBD, helping you compare value across all rounds — not just the early ones.

Positional Scarcity

Measures the drop-off from the elite tier to replacement level at each position. If QB has a small dropoff but TE has a huge one, you should prioritize TE — because the quality falls off a cliff after the top TEs are gone.

How it's calculated: For each position, OverADP finds the projected points of the #1 player (elite) and the projected points of the last starter that would be rostered across all teams (replacement). The difference is the scarcity score.

Scarcity LevelWhat It MeansDraft Strategy
High ScarcityHuge gap between elite and replacementDraft this position early — waiting means getting a much worse player
Medium ScarcityModerate dropoffBalanced — draft when value aligns with your pick
Low ScarcityShallow dropoff — many comparable optionsSafe to wait — replacement-level players are almost as good as "starters"

Example: In a 12-team league starting 1 TE, the #1 TE (Kittle, 136 pts) and the #12 TE (~97 pts) differ by 39 pts. But the #1 QB (Lamar, 245 pts) and #12 QB (~170 pts) differ by 75 pts. However, QB scarcity is often lower because there are only 12-14 starting QBs rostered, while TE has a steeper talent cliff at the top. The scarcity panel shows you which positions have the steepest cliffs so you can draft accordingly.

Confidence Intervals (CQR)

The 80% CI is built via Conformalized Quantile Regression — we train separate quantile models at the 10th and 90th percentiles, then apply a conformal adjustment learned on a held-out calibration season to guarantee marginal coverage.

Empirical coverage (target 80%, held-out 2024): QB 88%, RB 79%, WR 86%, TE 80% — average 83.5%. Naïve quantile regression alone only covers 56%; the conformal step is what makes the intervals honest.

Rookies and 2nd-year players get a 1.8× / 1.3× width multiplier on top of the calibrated interval to reflect their scarcer historical data.

Model vs ADP

Our model explains 58% of variance in fantasy points on held-out seasons. ADP alone (a linear projection from draft position) explains 15% — nearly 4× worse. The breakdown by position on walk-forward validation (train 2019 to N-1, test season N, averaged across 2022-2025):

Handcuff Alerts

A handcuff is a backup RB on the same NFL team as your starting RB. If your starter gets injured, their backup typically takes over the starting role and inherits most of the carries — making them extremely valuable as insurance.

Why it matters: If you drafted Saquon Barkley in the 1st round and he goes down in Week 3, his backup (who you can draft in the last round for almost nothing) suddenly becomes a top-15 RB. Without the handcuff, you're scrambling on waivers.

Handcuff TypeDescriptionPriority
Must HandcuffYour RB1's backup — protect your 1st/2nd round investmentHigh — draft in late rounds
ConsiderYour RB2/RB3's backup — nice insurance but less criticalMedium — if bench space allows
AvailableOther backup RBs still on the boardLow — only if you have empty bench slots

Strategy: Draft your stud RB's handcuff with one of your last picks. It's like buying insurance — you hope you never need it, but if you do, it saves your season. The Handcuff Alerts panel shows you which backups are still available for RBs already on your roster.

Model Architecture

OverADP trains four base models per position (Ridge, Random Forest, XGBoost, CatBoost) and picks the best per-position model via walk-forward validation rather than naively averaging. In the current production build, CatBoost wins RB and WR; XGBoost wins QB and TE. All CatBoost models use per-position tuned hyperparameters, temporal sample weighting (more recent seasons matter more), and monotonic constraints on aggregate production lags (more prior-year points → never a lower projection).

Calibrated confidence intervals come from a parallel Conformalized Quantile Regression pipeline (see Confidence Intervals section above).

ModelRole
CatBoost (tuned)Wins RB & WR. Ordered boosting + monotonic constraints.
XGBoostWins QB & TE. Gradient boosting with strong tabular performance.
Random ForestEnsemble candidate + uncertainty signal (not typically selected).
RidgeLinear baseline + stability anchor.

Key Features (April 2026)

  • Depth chart role — Week 1 nflverse snapshot, with depth_rank, is_starter, is_backup for QB/WR/TE (RB skipped due to RBBC noise). Refreshable post-draft/cuts via scripts/refresh_depth_charts.py.
  • Target / carry competition — Sum of teammate prior-year production on current roster. Captures FA moves and scheme shifts directly.
  • College & draft pipeline — Draft capital, combine athleticism, college per-game production, Power 5 flags. Projects rookies before they play a snap.
  • Injury-adjusted regression — Separates injury-driven declines from talent-driven declines; flags bounce-back candidates.
  • Conformal 80% CIs — 83.5% empirical coverage on held-out test; 28-71% for the old ensemble-std approach.
  • ADP matching — Robust via football_name (handles apostrophes, suffixes, nicknames).

Data Sources

  • nflverse (via nfl_data_py) — Seasonal stats, rosters, team stats, OL metrics, snap counts, injuries, depth charts
  • Fantasy Football Calculator — ADP (half-PPR) and bye weeks
  • FantasyPros — K and DEF projections (ML is not reliable for these; we use consensus instead)
  • Pro-Football-Reference — Combine data, draft capital, college production stats
